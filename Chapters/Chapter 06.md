**Chapter 6

Dualistic Sliders: Approach / Avoid**

Everything you do is shaped by two basic signals:

Move toward.
Move away.

That’s it.

Every preference, reaction, craving, and resistance reduces to this structure.

In your system, these are sliders.

Not moral issues.
Not personality flaws.
Not character defects.

Control signals.


---

The Approach / Avoid Axis

Every object in experience triggers one of three responses:

Move toward it
Move away from it
Leave it alone

Approach and avoid are not “good” and “bad.”

They are directional instructions.

They tell the system where to send energy.


---

Liking and Disliking as Amplifiers

When you say:

“I like this.”

You are telling the system:

Increase signal.
Increase proximity.
Increase repetition.

That is positive amplification.

When you say:

“I don’t like this.”

You are telling the system:

Push away.
Suppress.
Remove.

That is also amplification.

Because pushing requires energy.


---

Why Both Add Energy

Most people think disliking reduces energy.

It doesn’t.

It applies energy in the opposite direction.

Trying to suppress a thought takes effort.
Trying to resist a feeling creates tension.
Trying not to care requires work.

“I don’t want this” is still engagement.

It is still gain applied to the object.


---

Positive and Negative Feedback Loops

Positive feedback loop:

Like → Seek → Reward → Like more

Negative feedback loop:

Dislike → Resist → Tension → Dislike more

Both are loops.
Both sustain activation.
Neither is neutral.


---

The Trap of “I Don’t Like This”

Watch closely.

“I don’t like this person.”
“I don’t like this feeling.”
“I don’t like this situation.”

The body tightens.
Attention locks.
Narrative builds.

Energy rises.

You didn’t weaken the signal.

You reinforced it.

Avoidance is counter-force.

Not attenuation.


---

The Slider Model

Each dualistic pair is a slider:

+10 = Maximum approach
0   = Neutral
–10 = Maximum avoidance

Most people live between +8 and –8.

High spikes.
Hard crashes.
Chronic drama.

The system overheats.


---

The +1 / –1 Life

Stability lives near center.

+1 = mild preference
–1 = mild preference

Enough signal to act.
Not enough to destabilize.

You still enjoy.
You still dislike.

But the oscillation is small.

That’s sustainable.

This is life lived near zero:
balanced tone with live gain.


---

Gain vs Slider (Critical Distinction)

Sliders are direction.
Gain is global force — the volume of energy in the system.

Example:

+2 approach at low gain → gentle interest
+2 approach at high gain → obsession

–2 avoid at low gain → mild boundary
–2 avoid at high gain → rage

Same slider.
Different pressure.

Most people try to fix the object.

They should fix the force.


---

Sliders Create Momentum

This is rarely taught.

Moving a slider creates motion.

Like steering a vehicle.

Push forward → you move forward.
Turn left → you start turning.

Inputs create momentum.

Momentum continues
after you stop pushing.


---

Why People Oscillate

Untrained systems do this:

+5 → overshoot → –6 → overshoot → +7 → crash

Push.
Panic.
Counter-push.
Overcorrect.

Life becomes constant recovery.

No stability.


---

The Egg-Grasp Problem

Modern robots can pick up an egg.

To do that, they must:

Locate it
Align with it
Apply force
Monitor feedback
Adjust continuously

Too little force → drop.
Too much force → crush.

Human regulation is the same.

Relationships, pricing, boundaries, emotions:

You are always gripping something.

With too much force.
Or too little.

Training teaches calibrated grip.


---

Isometric Balance (Holding a Position)

Think of holding your hand steady.

You activate:

Flexors and extensors
Push and pull
Stabilize and release

At the same time.

That is isometric control.

Power without movement.

The nervous system does this too.

When trained, it can:

Hold +3 interest without obsession
Hold –3 boundary without rage
Hold engagement without collapse

This is active balance.

Not slack.
Not force.

Control.


---

How Stability Actually Works

To hold any setting, you need:

1. Direction (slider)


2. Force (gain)


3. Damping (SBR)



Calibration sets the baseline.
SBR holds it.
Sliders steer it.

Remove any one:

No direction → drift
No force → collapse
No damping → oscillation

All three are required.


---

Landing a Setting: The SBR Sequence

When you want to hold a position:

1. Set the slider


2. Raise gain slightly (to stop drift)


3. Stare — contain


4. Breathe — synchronize


5. Relax — discharge excess



Then the system settles.

Like leveling a helicopter.

Without this:

You overshoot.
You wobble.
You burn energy.


---

Craving as Containment (Without Mysticism)

In early regulation, you often use effort.

You “hold yourself.”

You clamp attention.
You restrain impulses.
You prevent spirals.

This is not failure.

It is scaffolding.

With training, less force is needed.

Containment becomes natural.


---

Practical Application

When you feel pulled hard:

Ask:

Which slider is active?
How far from zero?
What is my gain?
Is there momentum?

Usually:

Lower gain.
Stabilize.
Then adjust.

Not the reverse.


---

The Hidden Insight

Approach and avoid are not the problem.

Unregulated force and unmanaged momentum are.

When gain is stable and SBR is trained:

Sliders become tools.

When gain is high and SBR is weak:

Sliders become tyrants.


---

What Mastery Looks Like

A trained system can:

Engage without obsession
Disengage without collapse
Care without clinging
Set boundaries without anger

That is not temperament.

That is skill.

### The Third Signal — The Idle Hum

You now understand the two primary gain mechanisms.

Approach gain — the system pulling toward something pleasant.
Avoidance gain — the system pushing away from something unpleasant.

Both have objects.
Both have direction.
Both become visible once you know what to look for.

But there is a third signal.

And most people never see it.

Not because it is hidden.

Because it has no object.
No direction.
No obvious pull.

It is simply: on.

---

### What the Idle Hum Is

After you have worked with approach and avoidance for a while,
something unexpected happens.

Both of them quiet down.

The reaching stops.
The bracing stops.

And the system keeps running.

Not dramatically.
Not urgently.

Just a background hum.
A subtle readiness.
A barely perceptible sense that something should happen next.

This is the idle signal.

The system maintaining its own activation
above the threshold required to keep the process going.

Not toward anything.
Not away from anything.

Just: on.

---

### Why It Is Hard to See

The first two signals have handles.

When approach gain is running hot,
there is an object you can point to.
There is a direction you can feel.

When avoidance gain is running hot,
there is a sensation you can identify.
There is a resistance you can locate.

The idle hum has no handle.

It is not toward anything specific.
It is not away from anything specific.

It is the power being on.

Approach and avoidance are specific currents
running in specific directions.

The idle hum is the electricity itself.

---

### How to Recognize It

You recognize the idle hum by process of elimination.

When approach gain is not running hot —
when avoidance gain is not running hot —
when the system is neither over nor under-energized —

and the system is still running —

that background running is the idle hum.

In ordinary life it feels like:

Mild restlessness without a clear object.

The sense that you should be doing something
even when there is nothing that needs doing.

A subtle forward lean — not toward anything specific,
just forward.

Difficulty being fully still even when everything is handled.

Opening your phone without knowing why.

Feeling vaguely unsatisfied even when nothing is wrong.

---

### Why It Matters

The idle hum is not a problem in ordinary function.

Some background activation is healthy.
It keeps the system responsive.

But when you are trying to genuinely rest,
recover, or make a clean decision from neutral,
the idle hum prevents full return to zero.

It is why people cannot truly stop even when they stop.

They sit down.
They put the phone down.
They have nowhere to be.

And the system keeps running anyway.

Not from craving anything specific.
Not from avoiding anything specific.

Just from the habit of being on.

This is also why the window of tolerance narrows over time
in people who never fully power down.

The resting state keeps rising.
The baseline keeps climbing.
Recovery becomes less complete with each cycle.

---

### The Relationship to Gain

The idle hum is a gain phenomenon, not a slider phenomenon.

Sliders are directional — they point toward or away from objects.

The idle hum is the master gain knob
refusing to come all the way down.

Even when sliders are at zero,
the system volume stays elevated.

This is why gain management is more fundamental
than slider management.

You can resolve both approach and avoidance
and still be dysregulated
if the idle hum is keeping the gain too high.

---

### What to Do With It

The idle hum responds to the same basic move as the other two signals.

Do not amplify it.
Do not resist it.
Do not find it interesting.

Simply do not add fuel.

This is different from suppression.

Suppression is pushing the hum away —
avoidance gain applied to the idle signal itself.
That feeds it rather than starving it.

Not adding fuel means:

The hum is present.
You notice it without engaging with it.
You neither reach toward it nor push it away.
You do not make it a project.

You simply let it run without refueling it.

When you stop adding fuel to a fire,
it does not extinguish immediately.

It continues for a while.

Then it naturally reduces.

The system settles toward its actual resting state.

---

### The Practical Test

Here is a simple test for the idle hum.

Sit somewhere quiet with nothing to do.

No phone.
No task.
No plan.

Give the system two minutes.

If approach gain activates — you will reach for something.
If avoidance gain activates — you will start to brace.

But if both of those remain quiet
and the system still feels like it is running —
like it is waiting for the next thing —
like something should happen —

that is the idle hum.

You have just located the third signal.

---

### Why This Comes Last

Approach gain is the loudest signal.
Most people recognize it first.

Avoidance gain is the second loudest.
It becomes visible shortly after.

The idle hum is quietest.
It only becomes visible after the first two have settled enough
to stop dominating the field.

This is why regulation training has a sequence.

You cannot see the idle hum
while approach and avoidance are running loud.

The instrument needs to be quieter first.

Then the background becomes audible.

---

### What Changes When You See It

Most people, once they can see the idle hum,
notice something unexpected.

They were never actually at zero.

What they thought was calm
was just a quieter version of running.

What they thought was rest
was just deceleration without stopping.

This is not failure.

It is resolution improving.

The instrument is becoming precise enough
to detect what it could not detect before.

And once it is detected, it becomes workable.

Not as something to fight.
Not as something to achieve.
Not as a destination to reach.

As a signal to stop feeding.

---

### A Note on Patience

The idle hum does not resolve quickly.

Approach and avoidance respond to recognition
relatively fast.

The idle hum is more like lowering the temperature of a room
than turning off a light switch.

You stop adding heat.
The room gradually cools.
It takes longer than you expect.
But it does cool.

The corrective is not a technique.

It is a sustained willingness
to stop refueling what does not need fuel.

Over time, genuine rest becomes possible.

Not the performance of rest.
Not rest with one eye open.

The actual thing.

---

### Summary

The three gain signals are:

**Approach gain** — pulling toward pleasant objects.
Corrective: reduce the pull. Stop amplifying.

**Avoidance gain** — pushing away from unpleasant objects.
Corrective: reduce the push. Stop amplifying.

**The idle hum** — background activation with no object.
No direction. Just: on.
Corrective: stop adding fuel. Let it run without refueling.
Allow it to settle toward actual zero.

All three sustain the system above its natural resting point.

All three respond to the same fundamental move:

Stop adding fuel.

The difference is that the first two have objects you can point to.

The third requires learning to see the field itself —
not what is in it,
but that it is running.

That is the subtler skill.

And it is available to anyone
who has learned to read the instrument carefully enough
to notice what remains
when everything obvious has already quieted.
