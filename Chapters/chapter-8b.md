# Chapter 8B

## Five Ways the System Loses Calibration

### (And How to Correct Each One)

---

### What This Chapter Is

You now know how to find baseline, detect drift, regulate gain,
and stay inside your window of tolerance.

That is the foundation.

But the system will still lose calibration.

Not because you failed.

Because that is what systems do under load.

The question is not whether calibration is lost.

It is whether you can recognize what happened
and apply the correct corrective.

There are exactly five ways the instrument loses calibration.

Not approximately five.
Not metaphorically five.

Five.

Each has a specific signature.
Each has a specific corrective.
None of them are moral failures.

They are mechanical states.

When you can name which one is active,
you are already most of the way back.

---

### The Diagnostic Question

When something feels off, stop asking:

*What is wrong with me?*

Start asking:

*Which of the five is active right now?*

That shift — from identity to mechanics — is the entire chapter.

---

### Failure Mode One — Approach Gain Running Hot

**What it is:**

The system is pulling toward something.

A pleasant outcome.
An interesting idea.
A person, purchase, or possibility.
A state you want to stay in.

Gain is being applied in the approach direction
and it has exceeded the workable threshold.

**What it feels like:**

Restless. Reaching. Hungry for the next thing.

Sometimes it feels like excitement that keeps escalating.
Sometimes it feels like obsession with a small decision.
Sometimes it feels pleasant but will not settle.

The signature is: *something keeps pulling the cursor forward.*

**Common appearances in ordinary life:**

Checking the same thing repeatedly.
Reopening a decision you already made.
Researching past the point of useful information.
Escalating a plan beyond what the situation requires.
Spending more than you intended.
Saying yes when you meant to pause.

**The corrective:**

Reduce approach gain.

Soften the grip.
Let the object remain without reaching toward it.
Release the forward lean.

You are not destroying the pleasant object.
You are withdrawing the amplification.

This is not suppression.
Suppression pushes the object away.
This simply removes the pull.

The object can still be there.
You just stop amplifying it.

---

### Failure Mode Two — Avoidance Gain Running Hot

**What it is:**

The system is pushing away from something.

An uncomfortable conversation.
An unpleasant task.
A feeling you do not want to have.
A situation that feels threatening.

Gain is being applied in the avoidance direction
and it has exceeded the workable threshold.

**What it feels like:**

Tight. Braced. Resistant.

Sometimes it feels like low-level irritation with no clear cause.
Sometimes it feels like dread about something specific.
Sometimes it feels like the body is holding its breath.

The signature is: *something keeps pushing the cursor backward.*

**Common appearances in ordinary life:**

Delaying a call or email you know you need to send.
Avoiding a room, person, or topic.
Feeling suddenly busy when something difficult appears.
Snapping at people near the avoided thing.
Cleaning the house instead of starting the project.
Saying you are fine when you are not.

**The corrective:**

Reduce avoidance gain.

Soften the resistance.
Let the unpleasant object remain without bracing against it.
Release the backward lean.

This is not forcing yourself to accept something bad.
That would be suppression wearing a different name.

This is simply withdrawing the push.

The object can remain exactly as it is.
You just stop amplifying the resistance.

---

### The Symmetry Between One and Two

Both failure modes feel completely different.

Reaching is not the same as bracing.
Hunger is not the same as dread.

But mechanically they are identical.

Both are gain applied to a formation in one direction.
Both destabilize in the same way.
Both respond to the same basic corrective: reduce gain.

This is why craving and aversion feel like opposites
but are structurally the same problem.

When you notice either one, the question is the same:

*How much gain am I applying here, and can I reduce it?*

---

### Failure Mode Three — System Under-Energized

**What it is:**

The system is below its minimum operating threshold.

Not pulling toward anything.
Not pushing away from anything.

Just low.

**What it feels like:**

Heavy. Foggy. Dull.

Thoughts move slowly or stop entirely.
The body feels dense.
Attention keeps sliding off whatever you aim it at.
Sleep pressure increases even if you are not tired.
Everything feels distant or muffled.

The signature is: *the instrument cannot hold an object.*

**Common appearances in ordinary life:**

Knowing exactly what you need to do and being unable to start.
Staring at a task without beginning.
Reading the same sentence three times.
Waiting for motivation that does not arrive.
Scrolling instead of working.
Canceling things that would actually help.

**The corrective:**

Add energy carefully.

The key word is carefully.

The most common mistake here is overcorrecting —
adding too much energy and tipping into Failure Mode Four.

Safe ways to add energy:

- Mild physical movement — standing, walking, light isometric tension
- Genuine interest directed at one specific thing
- Slightly more precise attention on a simple object
- Changing environment briefly
- Returning to ordinary activity if the system will not stabilize

Interest is the cleanest tool.

Not force. Not stimulation. Not caffeine as a substitute for regulation.

Just a genuine noticing of something specific.

The system needs gentle re-engagement,
not a jump-start.

---

### Failure Mode Four — System Over-Energized

**What it is:**

The system is above its maximum stable threshold.

**What it feels like:**

Agitated. Scattered. Pressured.

Thoughts move too fast to follow.
Attention jumps between objects without completing a cycle.
The body feels buzzy or tight.
There is a sense of urgency without a clear object.
Everything feels slightly too loud and slightly too close.

The signature is: *the instrument cannot stay with an object.*

**Common appearances in ordinary life:**

Opening five tabs and completing none.
Starting tasks and abandoning them mid-step.
Sending messages you later regret.
Making decisions too quickly and reversing them.
Feeling like there is not enough time even when there is.
Interpreting neutral events as threats.

**The corrective:**

Reduce system energy.

Release excess tension in the body.
Soften the breath — especially the exhale.
Widen attention rather than narrowing it further.
Let the system slow without forcing it.

The Seven Gain Factors apply here directly.

When the system is too high, emphasize:
comfort, concentration, equanimity.

When the system is too low, emphasize:
investigation, persistence, interest.

If you cannot tell which direction you are off,
use the relaxation diagnostic from Chapter 2.

Relax and observe.

If the system stabilizes — you were over-energized.
If the system collapses — you were under-energized.

One move. Clear answer.

---

### Failure Mode Five — Decision Mechanism Seized

**What it is:**

The volitional system locks.

The basic control surface — move toward, move away, release —
becomes simultaneously unavailable.

The system cannot move forward.
Cannot move backward.
Cannot let go.

**What it feels like:**

It presents as doubt.

Not ordinary uncertainty about which option is better.

Catastrophic doubt.

The kind that says:

*Nothing I do is right.*
*I cannot know anything for certain.*
*Every option is equally wrong.*
*I should wait until I am sure — but I am never sure.*

The system freezes.
Heat builds.
Time passes.
Nothing moves.

The signature is: *paralysis presenting as careful consideration.*

**Common appearances in ordinary life:**

Reopening the same decision multiple times after it was made.
Asking for opinions from so many people that the answers cancel out.
Researching past the point where new information would change anything.
Feeling unable to send something even though you know it is ready.
Preparing extensively without ever beginning.
Staying in situations that are clearly not working
because leaving feels equally impossible.

**Why this failure mode is the most dangerous:**

It masquerades as wisdom.

It looks like thoroughness.
It sounds like humility.
It feels like responsibility.

But functionally it is seizure.

The decision mechanism is not deliberating.
It is locked.

And unlike the other four failure modes,
this one can sustain itself indefinitely
because waiting always feels justified.

**The corrective:**

A small reversible action. Any action.

Not the right action.
Not the perfect action.
Not the action you will never regret.

A small action with limited consequences that can be adjusted afterward.

The volitional mechanism needs to complete one cycle
to break the seizure.

The content of the action is almost irrelevant.
The completion is what matters.

This is what PEP #4 is for.

The 51/49 rule is not a decision tool.
It is a seizure breaker.

You assign 51% to one option —
not because it is better
but because the cycle needs to complete.

Then you move.
Then you observe.
Then you adjust.

**What if I make a mistake?**

You probably will.

That is acceptable.

A mistake with consequences is recoverable.
A seized system accumulating heat indefinitely is not.

Move. Adjust. Move again.

### The Complete Diagnostic Map

When something feels off, ask:
*What is the instrument doing right now?*

The answer is always one of these:

**One — Approach gain running hot**
Signature: pulling, reaching, restless escalation, cannot settle
Corrective: reduce approach gain, soften the grip, stop amplifying

**Two — Avoidance gain running hot**
Signature: tight, braced, resistant, avoiding, snapping
Corrective: reduce avoidance gain, release the push, stop amplifying

**Three — Under-energized**
Signature: heavy, foggy, dull, attention slides, cannot start
Corrective: add energy carefully through gentle interest and movement

**Four — Over-energized**
Signature: scattered, pressured, agitated, attention jumps, everything urgent
Corrective: reduce energy through release, soften breath, widen attention

**Five — Decision mechanism seized**
Signature: paralysis disguised as careful doubt, loops without resolution
Corrective: small reversible action to complete one volitional cycle

---

### When Failure Modes Combine

More than one failure mode can be active simultaneously.

This is common.

**Over-energized plus approach gain running hot:**

The signature is obsession.

The system is flooded with energy
and all of it is aimed at one object.

Thoughts loop. Sleep suffers.
The object expands to fill the whole frame.

Corrective: reduce system energy first,
then address the approach gain.

Always lower overall gain before adjusting sliders.

**Under-energized plus decision mechanism seized:**

The signature is depression.

The system lacks power and cannot direct what little it has.

Nothing starts. Nothing resolves.
Waiting feels like the only option
even when the costs of waiting are visible.

Corrective: add energy through movement and environment change first,
then use a minimal symmetry break to restart the volitional cycle.

**Avoidance gain plus over-energization:**

The signature is panic.

The system is flooded and everything feels threatening.

The world narrows.
Flight feels necessary.
Danger feels everywhere.

Corrective: physical release first — movement, breath, posture.
Then widen attention to reduce the narrowing.
Then address the avoidance gain.

---

### The Sequence That Works

When you recognize a failure mode, the sequence is always:

1. Name it without story.
   Not: *I'm such a mess.*
   Just: *Over-energized. Failure Mode Four.*

2. Lower overall gain first if it is high.
   Almost every failure mode is made worse by high gain.
   Reduce system intensity before adjusting anything else.

3. Apply the specific corrective for the active mode.

4. Check again.
   Did the instrument recover?
   Or is there a second failure mode underneath?

5. Re-enter activity.

---

### What You Are Not Doing

You are not:

Judging yourself for having a failure mode active.
Performing calm.
Suppressing what is present.
Waiting until you feel ready.

You are:

Identifying a mechanical state.
Applying a mechanical corrective.
Returning the instrument to workable calibration.

That is all.

---

### The Shift This Creates

Before this chapter, difficulty in practice felt personal.

*Something is wrong with me.*
*I cannot do this.*
*I am broken.*

After this chapter, difficulty in practice becomes diagnostic.

*Failure Mode Three. Under-energized.*
*Add gentle interest. Movement. Then re-engage.*

That shift — from identity to mechanics — changes everything.

Not because the difficulty disappears.

Because you know what it is.

And you know what to do.

---

### Safety Note

The corrective for every failure mode is gentle.

If approach gain is running hot,
you do not violently suppress the reaching.
You soften it.

If the system is over-energized,
you do not force it down.
You release excess tension and let it settle.

If decision seizure is present,
you do not berate yourself for being paralyzed.
You take one small action.

Force created most of these problems.

Gentleness corrects almost all of them.

If gentleness does not work:

Return to baseline externally.
Stop the practice.
Return to ordinary activity.
Let the system reset through normal living.

Nothing essential is lost by stopping.

Everything essential is lost by forcing.
